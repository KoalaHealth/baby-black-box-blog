    The mother’s vaginal and pelvic health is something few new parents discuss, but it is very important and beneficial to discuss before symptoms arise.

    Photo by Timothy Meinberg on Unsplash

    I work at a women’s clinic at one of the world’s top women’s hospitals. I have the opportunity to learn and observe the worst of what happens when deliveries go poorly and when women delay their care.

    Here are a list of things that can affect women’s pelvic region in their lifetimes: cysctocele, enterocele, rectocele, SUI, UUI, overflow incontinence, UTI, vaginal dryness, tearing, and pelvic pain.

    If this is a new topic to you, and you insist on seeing something you cannot unsee, look here: Female Pelvic Organ Prolapse (p.s. I have seen worse).

    About ~90% of my co-workers are women and most are mothers. They find solace in telling me on the near-daily about all of their personal health issues - after delivery. In return, I quietly listen, receive permission, anonymize, and share them with you, my friends. Here are some things I’ve taken away:

    1. Women’s vaginas and breasts are important parts of their self-identity and sense of feminity.
    • Remember: You love your partner, all of our bodies change, and you will support them, especially in their time of need.
    • Strategy: Remind and verbalize how much you love them. Show them those natural body changes do not matter, and do not matter to you.

    1. We always hope for the best, but spontaneous miscarriage is not uncommon.
    • Strategy: Set aside your pain and fake it till you make it. You should be there to provide support. Be first to show affection (this does not necessarily mean sex). If your partner likes kisses, kiss them. If they like hugs, hug them. It’s about being present, and not letting the loss escalate and snowball.

    1. Post-partum depression is real. It seemed like every mother at the clinic reported post-partum depression.
    • 1 in 8 mothers across the United States and 1 in 5 (20%!) experience post-partum depression in some areas.
    • Up to 75% of new mothers experience shifts in emotions following delivery (aka “baby blues”).
    • Important: Ask your physicians about the local resources available to you and recommendations on how you can prepare. If your partner is in need of support and you are caring for your first newborn, you will likely not have the energy, time, and soundness of mind to gather this information.

    1. Most women’s health advice are passed down from grandmother to mother to daughter…
      (Cross your legs before you laugh/sneeze/cough, insert an assortment of fruit into your vagina, hang upside down on monkey bars, etc.). Though we discourage inserting a pomegranate into a vagina today, this type of knowledge was well guarded by the female members of a family.
    • In our society, this is often taboo, but for your partner’s sake, you may want to discuss these things or facilitate a discussion with a gynecologist.
    • Strategy: Major academic hospitals (e.g. Mayo Clinic, Cleveland Clinic, Brigham Women’s Hospital, UCSF, Stanford) often hire excellent gynecologists. Consider starting the discussion with recommendations on how you can support.

    1. Sooner or later, vaginas become dry. The FAR post-partum mothers may want to discuss vaginal estrogen (premarin/estradiol) with they gynecologists. The general tissue quality (decreased muscle tone, elasticity issues, dryness, etc.) in the pelvic region goes down.

    2. The pelvic floor muscles and connective tissue hold up the organs in the abdomen.

    3. Kegels can be done wrong, but it’s better to do them wrong than not do them at all.
