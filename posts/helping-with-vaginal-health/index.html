The mother’s vaginal and pelvic health is something few new parents discuss, but it is very important and beneficial to discuss before symptoms arise. Part II

Photo by Timothy Meinberg on Unsplash

I haven’t lost you yet? Wow mom! Well, here’s the gold: what you can do to support your partner!

  1. I cannot stress this enough. Consult a professional pelvic floor physical therapist (PFPT). In the U.S., these healthcare specialists are accredited by the Academy of Pelvic Health Physical Therapy. The APTA physical therapist search tool is terrible, but one of the few up-to-date directories. Go here to try to find a PFPT in your area: APTA PFPT Directory.
  • Motivation: Our obstetrician told my wife she is “wall-to-wall” baby, and our entire healthcare team was blown away when she did not have any tearing after delivery. Zero stitches and her stomach deflated almost immediately upon delivery. My wife and I truly believe pelvic floor physical therapy during pregnancy played a factor.

  1. I AM NOT A PELVIC FLOOR PHYSICAL THERAPIST. If you cannot access a PFPT (there aren’t many of them), you and your partner may want to consider at-home perineal massage.
  • Do not try this without consulting a licensed medical professional. There are considerations such as genital herpes, thrush, and other infections affecting the vaginal region.
  • If you and your partner understand what gentle means for the other, you may want to read this information (PDF) from Oxford University Hosptial’s guide to self-performed perineal massage.
  • If you are requesting your partner’s help, the partner is recommended to use index fingers. GO GENTLY, SLOWLY, and SMOOTHLY. LISTEN TO YOUR PARTNER AND HER CUES.
  • I recommend washing your hands, turning on some calming music, and lowering the lights. Use lubrication. Don’t go longer than 2 minutes. If you are unsure, err on the side of being too soft.

  1. Exercise with your partner. Let’s look at the numbers:
  • Women with primary family members (e.g. sisters, mothers) with POP had 2.58% greater likelihood of POP than those without (a statistically significant OR/odds ratio). This indicates pelvic organ prolapse (either the bladder, vagina, or rectum falling down) seems to be genetic.
  • An epidemiological approach shows caucasian and asian women are far more likely to develop POP than black women.
  • Obese women have 40-75% increased risk of developing pelvic organ prolapse. However, a 5% decrease in body weight can substantially reduce risks.
  • Note: Some physicians believe heavy lifting can increase risks, if you are in an at-risk (predisposed) group.
